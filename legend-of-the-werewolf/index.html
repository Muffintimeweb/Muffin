by Yappo · August 5, 2017

The term waswolf, or lycanthrope (lykánthrôpos of the Greek lúkos, loup and ánthrôpos, human being), refers to a human being who has the faculty of transforming himself into a wolf, voluntarily or involuntarily. The metamorphosis usually occurs during the nights of full moon, forcing the lycanthropus to wander in its wolf form until the first rays of the sun.

In its modern version, the werewolf presents all the physical characteristics of the wolf: long hairs completely cover the body and it is suddenly provided with fantastic muscular power, long fangs and sharp claws. The werewolf screams to the moon like a wolf, and if he sometimes stands on two legs, like a man, it is not an absolute rule. When he changes his appearance, his sense of smell, hearing and night vision develop. He becomes agile, cunning, and shows incredible speed. The werewolf is a lonely and merciless killer. He always shows an implacable ferocity and massacres without distinction the animals and the men whom he crosses at random from his nocturnal outings in order to regain their flesh. Like the vampires,

The Metamorphosis of Lycaon en Loup

Lycanthropy could be the result of a divine punishment as we learn from the legend of King Lycaon, the first and oldest of the famous werewolves. In Greek mythology, Lycaon was king of Arcadia. He was known to be a cruel king who respected nothing and nobody and was known for his disdain for the gods. In order to test him, Zeus, accompanied by other gods, presented himself to him in the guise of a beggar.
Lycaon, scenting the trap, invited the beggars to eat at his table. But, desiring to verify their divine nature, he caused his grandson, Arcas, to be killed and offered it to the gods as a meal.
All the gods immediately perceived it, except Demeter, who mistakenly ate a shoulder. Unworthy, Zeus then thundered the king’s fifty sons and then changed Lycaon into a wolf. The young Arcas was resurrected and Zeus offered him the throne of his grandfather. When her shoulder was missing, she was replaced by a silver shoulder.
Following this legend, cannibalism was closely associated with lycanthropy in ancient Greece, where it was also believed that devouring the raw flesh of an enraged wolf transformed into a lycanthropist.

On Mount Lykaion, the birthplace of Zeus, games were held every four years. They were accompanied by banquets in honor of the god, during which practices of cannibalism were common. The human flesh was divided between the different participants who were then changed into wolves for nine years. If they did not consume any human flesh during this period, they could then recover their original form.
Quoting Agriopas, Pliny the Elder reported the story of the Demenet of Parrhasia, who, having tasted the entrails of a child, sacrificed at a sacrifice that the Arcadians had made to Jupiter, had been transformed into a wolf. At the end of ten years, he had returned to his human form and then went to the Games, where he had competed for the price of the pugilate. He had returned victorious from Olympia. In his writings Pliny the Elder also quoted Euanthes, a Greek writer who was not without reputation, who explained that according to the books of the Arcadians, a man from the family of a certain Anthus was chosen at random from among his own And that he was taken to a pond in Arcadia and then hung his clothes on an oak before crossing the pond, then he turned into a wolf and lived with them for nine years. If, during this period, he stood away from the man, then he could return to the pond and after crossing it, he recovered his human form, aged nine years. He indicated that he even found his old clothes.

During the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the beliefs remained perennial and the lawsuits for lycanthropy were very numerous. Those who expressed doubts as to the veracity of the phenomenon took the risk of being suspected of sympathy for the sorcerers. The skeptics were few and the doctors were very discreet. The werewolves were detested by the inquisitors, who mercilessly pursued them. A large number of unfortunate accused of lycanthropia were burned daily, and the theologians and devotees complained continually that they were not burning enough.

Henry Boguet, a notorious Inquisitor, had nine cases involving werewolves. He thought the werewolf was a direct manifestation of the Devil.
According to him, Satan abandoned the sleeping lycanthropus in a bush and brought out a wolf. The animal then committed crimes that haunted the mind of the sleeper and disturbed his imagination to such an extent that the lycanthropus had really believed he had metamorphosed into a wolf. He therefore thought he had run the campaign by massacring animals and human beings. Although the reality of physical transformation was questioned in this theory, the werewolf was still held accountable for his actions because he had agreed with the demon, which was an extremely serious crime.

Delancre reported this anecdote on a Duke of Russia, who, warned that his subject was changing into all sorts of animals, sent for him; And, after having chained him, commanded him to make an experiment of his art; Which he soon changed into a wolf; but this duke, having prepared two mastiffs, made them spring against this wretch, who was at once beaten to pieces.

Aristide Dey described the nature of the so-called werewolves thus:
“In remote times, where obscurantism and superstitions reigned, beings depraved by misery, exalted out of the ways of humanity by the use of intoxicating drugs, Covered themselves with the skin of a wolf, trained themselves to run four feet, and, throwing terror into the people in this disguise, lived through depradations and went so far as to feed on human flesh. ”

Lycanthropy, Famous Cases and Trials

Loup-Garou in Normandy

From the end of the Middle Ages to the Renaissance, over a hundred years ago, many reports of werewolf attacks and nearly 30,000 lycanthropic trials were recorded in France. It is estimated that the number of trials in Europe was 100,000 but according to Collin de Plancy, tens of thousands of other suspected lycanthropists would have perished without trial, victims of popular vindictiveness.

In some cases there was evidence of murder and cannibalism, without any association with wolves, and in other cases people were simply frightened by wolves. Those found guilty of lycanthropy were burnt at the stake or skinned alive, for legend had it that the hairs of the wolf were concealed under his skin. And sometimes, more rarely, they were hanged, quite simply. At the beginning of the seventeenth century, sorcery was condemned by James I of England and it was not until July 21, 1682, that trials of witchcraft were banned in France.

In 1182, Giraldus claimed to have discovered, in Ireland, a couple of Wolves. But we do not know more about this.

In 1440, Gilles de Rais was executed for acts of barbarism and lycanthropy.

In 1521, Pierre Burgeot and Michel Verdun were accused of lycanthropy by Jean Bodin, the prior of the Dominican convent of Poligny. The trial, which took place at Besancon, attracted a large crowd of curious. The accused were both convicted and burned alive. Their portraits remained posted in the village church to remind everyone what men were capable of doing when they were under the influence of the Devil.
In the same year, three men accused of lycanthropy were burned alive in Besancon. They had admitted that after they had rubbed themselves with fat they changed into wolves, mated with wolves, and devoured several children.

In 1541, in Pavia, Italy, a farmer was suspected of having shredded a large number of people with his teeth while in the form of a wolf. After his capture, he asserted that the only difference between a real wolf and him resided in the fur. She was pushing outward for a wolf, and inwardly for her.

In 1542, according to Henry Boguer, one hundred and fifty werewolves assembled in a square in Constantinople.

In 1573, during the autumn, when a terrible famine was raging, many crimes were committed on children whose bodies were found torn and devoured. Everything indicated that they had been attacked by wolves. The suspicions then turned to Gilles Garnier, a hermit who lived with his family in the middle of the forest of the Serre, in Armange. After being captured, the villagers took him to the Dole court. On 16 January 1574, after the suspect confessed to his crimes, the court delivered his verdict. Among the charges against him, he was accused, among others, of having consumed meat on a Friday, which was formally forbidden. “Gilles Garnier, who had fallen into witchcraft, had taken and taken several children between the ages of six and twelve, both with his hands as his paws and with his teeth,

In 1584, Pierre Gandillon and his son Georges were arrested and accused of having murdered and devoured many adolescents after smearing the body with an ointment. Their appearance had changed, they moved on all fours, they were provided with nails hard, thick and sharp as claws.

In 1588 Arline de Barioux was burned in the main square of Riom. He was accused of turning into a wolf every Friday afternoon to devour the children.

In 1598, in the Loire valley, Jacques Rollet, a simple-minded man who thought he was a wolf, had been discovered near the bloodied corpse of a 15-year-old boy. Jacques Rollet was already known as the werewolf of Caude.
According to various testimonies, he had fled the place of his crime before being found half naked in the woods. His long hair and big beard were tangled, and his blood-clad hands still held pieces of flesh. In his judgment, Jacques Rollet explained that he had killed judges, lawyers and bailiffs but that the flesh of the latter was hard and tasteless. The court had sentenced him to death but he was considered mentally defective and interned in an asylum where he had stayed for two years.

Peter Stumbb

In 1589 the trial and killing of Peter Stumbb, the most famous killer of the suspected werewolves, took place. He was known as the Werewolf of Bedburg. During his trial, he declared that the devil had given him a magic belt that allowed him to transform into a hungry wolf, a voracious, strong and powerful wolf, with large eyes that shone like fire in the night, a Large mouth with pointed and cruel teeth, a gigantic body and powerful legs. “.

From the same period, the story of a sanguinary tailor has reached us. The man disguised himself as a wolf and roamed the forests at dusk, attacking children and young people to open their throats. Sometimes he would attract the unfortunate in his shop, he would beat them and cut off their necks before cutting them into pieces, like butcher’s meat. At his execution he showed no remorse. The record of his trial was so horrible that the court decided not to keep it and ordered it to be destroyed.

In 1603, Jean Grenier of Aquitaine, a young boy of thirteen or fourteen years, was accused of lycanthropy. He was physically retarded and mentally deficient, but he was nevertheless considered responsible for the disappearance of children, including that of an infant in his cradle. After his arrest, he asserted that he was the son of a priest and admitted that he had devoured about fifteen children. In reality, his father was a farmman who was so violent towards him that the boy had fled to escape. Delivered to himself, he lived in a savage manner, subsisted through begging and worked occasionally.

In 1605 Henry Gandinn, a native of Limburg, was accused of turning into a werewolf using sorcery. He was transformed into a wolf in the company of two other men, one of whom was named Jan Wolf.
Henry Gardinn declared at his confession that the three men had attacked, killed and ate a child while they were in the form of a wolf. Henry Gardinn had been found guilty of witchcraft for the purpose of turning into a werewolf, and was condemned to be burned alive. During his arrest, his alleged accomplice, Jan the Wolf, had left the parish, but was arrested two years later and executed in turn.

In 1651 Hans appeared before the tribunal of Idavare. He was accused of being a werewolf for his eighteen years. Hans confessed to being a werewolf for two years. He claimed that he had obtained the body of a wolf by a man in black. When the judges asked him if his body had really taken part in the hunt, or if it was his soul that had been transmuted, Hans replied that he had found on his leg the traces of bites that a dog had made him when ‘He was a werewolf.

In 1692, at Jurgenburg, in Livonia, Thiess testified under oath that he and the other werewolves were the dogs of God. He claimed that they were warriors who had gone to hell with witches and demons. Their efforts had ensured that the Devil and his henchmen did not carry the grain of the last harvests into hell.
Thiess was firm in his assertions, saying that werewolves in Germany and Russia had also fought the devil’s henchmen in their own versions of hell. He maintained that when the werewolves died, their souls were welcomed into heaven in thanksgiving for their service. Thiess was finally sentenced to ten lashes for idolatry and superstitious belief.

Between 1500 and 1700, about 100,000 people were sentenced for lycanthropy and burned alive. During the 17th century, doctors’ explanations of lycanthropy put an end to most tortures and killings. However, the population remained fearful and preferred supernatural explanations. Many instances of animal attacks on herds or men inevitably resurfaced the legend of the werewolf.
Nowadays, there are always people who sincerely believe themselves to be werewolves and others who think that lycanthropes exist and that some human beings really turn into wolves.

The Gevaudan’s beast

In 1764, more than 300 people were victims of the Beast of Gevaudan, which raged in Lozère and the surrounding area. She was described as a giant wolf or a werewolf. His story inspired many theories.

In July 1766, the Quebec Gazette announced that a werewolf was running in the county of Kamouraska.

In 1766, the beast of Sarlat, in the Dordogne, massacred fifteen people.

Towards the middle of the 19th century, in a small village in Poland, a werewolf took away the most beautiful girl in the village.

In 1804, a man named Maréchal, accused of lycanthropy, was condemned to the galleys.

In 1853, in Spain, Manuel Blanco Romasanta admitted to killing thirteen people because he suffered from a curse that transformed him into a wolf.

In 1887, a wild coyote terrorized miners in Saskatchewan, Canada. He was struck down with a golden bullet, and the object was extracted from his body. The story was reported by miners to the writer Ed Bodin in 1940.

In 1899, in Pennsylvania, a peaceful old man had a reputation for being a werewolf.

Shortly before the First World War, three werewolves roamed the Belgian Ardennes.
At the same time, in Scotland, a shepherd from the neighborhood of Inverness was suspected of lycanthropy.

In 1925, a young boy from an Alsatian village was accused of being a werewolf.

In 1930, a werewolf was raging in the region of Bourg-la-Reine.

In 1946, a mysterious creature with all the characteristics of the werewolf created terror in a Navajo reserve in North America.

In 1946-1947 a mysterious animal attacked many herds in the canton of Valais, Switzerland. The affair was never elucidated.

In 1949, in Rome, the police had to investigate a strange affair: every month, at the full moon, a man of the city was in the grip of strange hallucinations and uttered sinister howlings.

In 1957, English newspapers reported the sad story of a seventeen-year-old man from the village of Eccles Hall who thought he was going to turn into a werewolf. To put an end to the moral suffering he endured, he had plunged a knife into his heart. The inquest opened after his death revealed that the unfortunate man had telephoned one of his friends before performing his gesture: “He told me that his face and his hands were changing colors and that he was Become a werewolf. Then he stopped, and then I heard grunts. ”

In Singapore, again in 1957, mysterious aggressions defeated the police. The rumor was that the attackers were werewolves who were attacking the residents of a nursing home. One night, one of the nurses had awakened with a start to see a horrible bestial face whose hair was so low on the forehead that they reached the root of the nose and whose mouth left sharp fangs. The mystery was never elucidated.

In 1975-1976, the Beast of the Vosges who killed many domestic animals was never identified. She reappeared in 1994 and she slaughtered eighty animals.

In 1978, in the south of Brazil, Rosario do Sul, a 16-year-old schoolgirl, was plagued by demonic visions. She asserted that the spirit of a ferocious wolf had seized her.

In 1982, in the Creuse, a carnivorous creature was at the origin of a series of attacks on flocks. The corpses of the animals, including a bull and a heifer of 400 kg, were horribly shredded. In an organized battle in Noth Forest in November 1982, a hunter claimed to have confronted the animal without successfully identifying it. The case has never been elucidated.
